# Forward-Start Options Under Heston

## Introduction

A **forward-start option** is an option whose strike is set at a future date, typically as a percentage of the underlying asset price at that date. For instance, a forward-start call might have payoff $(S_T - \alpha S_{T_1})^+$ where $T_1 < T$ is the **strike-setting date** and $\alpha$ is the **moneyness ratio** (e.g., $\alpha = 1$ for ATM, $\alpha = 1.1$ for 10% OTM). Forward-start options are building blocks of **cliquet** (ratchet) contracts, which chain together consecutive forward-start options.

Under the Black--Scholes model, forward-start options have a simple closed form because the distribution of $S_T / S_{T_1}$ is independent of $S_{T_1}$. Under the Heston model, this independence fails because the variance state $v_{T_1}$ at the strike-setting date is random and affects the subsequent distribution. However, the **conditional characteristic function** of $\ln(S_T / S_{T_1})$ given $v_{T_1}$ retains the affine Heston structure, enabling semi-analytical pricing via iterated Fourier inversion.

This section develops the pricing theory for forward-start options under Heston, derives the conditional characteristic function, analyzes the **forward implied volatility smile**, and connects the results to cliquet pricing.

## Forward-Start Option Payoff

### Definition

A **forward-start European call** with strike-setting date $T_1$, maturity $T > T_1$, and moneyness ratio $\alpha > 0$ has payoff:

$$
\Phi = \left(S_T - \alpha S_{T_1}\right)^+
$$

The strike $K = \alpha S_{T_1}$ is not known until $T_1$. A **forward-start put** has payoff $(\alpha S_{T_1} - S_T)^+$.

The price at time $0$ is:

$$
V_0 = e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[\left(S_T - \alpha S_{T_1}\right)^+\right]
$$

### Normalized Return

Define the **forward return** $R = S_T / S_{T_1}$. Then:

$$
\Phi = S_{T_1}(R - \alpha)^+
$$

and the price becomes:

$$
V_0 = e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[S_{T_1}(R - \alpha)^+\right]
$$

By the tower property of conditional expectation:

$$
V_0 = e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[S_{T_1} \, \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[(R - \alpha)^+ \mid \mathcal{F}_{T_1}\right]\right]
$$

The inner expectation is a European call on $R$ with strike $\alpha$, conditional on the information at $T_1$.

---

## Black--Scholes Case: Independence

### The Simplification

Under Black--Scholes ($v_t = \sigma^2$ constant), the forward return $R = S_T / S_{T_1}$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_{T_1}$ because:

$$
\ln R = \left(r - q - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)(T - T_1) + \sigma(W_T - W_{T_1})
$$

The increment $W_T - W_{T_1}$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_{T_1}$. Therefore:

$$
\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[(R - \alpha)^+ \mid \mathcal{F}_{T_1}\right] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[(R - \alpha)^+\right] = C_{\text{BS}}(1, \alpha, \sigma, r-q, T-T_1)
$$

where $C_{\text{BS}}$ is the Black--Scholes formula with spot $= 1$ and strike $= \alpha$. Since this is a constant:

$$
V_0 = e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_{T_1}] \cdot C_{\text{BS}}(1, \alpha, \sigma, r-q, T-T_1) = S_0 e^{-qT_1} \cdot e^{-r(T-T_1)} C_{\text{BS}}(1, \alpha, \sigma, r-q, T-T_1)
$$

The forward-start option price is simply the European call price (with unit spot and strike $\alpha$) scaled by $S_0 e^{-qT_1}$.

## Heston Case: Conditional Characteristic Function

### Why Independence Fails

Under Heston, the forward return depends on $\mathcal{F}_{T_1}$ through the **variance state** $v_{T_1}$:

$$
\ln R = \int_{T_1}^{T}\left(r - q - \frac{v_s}{2}\right) ds + \int_{T_1}^{T} \sqrt{v_s} \, dW_s^{(1)}
$$

The variance path $(v_s)_{s \in [T_1, T]}$ depends on $v_{T_1}$, which is $\mathcal{F}_{T_1}$-measurable. Consequently, the distribution of $R$ is **not independent** of $\mathcal{F}_{T_1}$ --- it depends on $v_{T_1}$.

### The Conditional CF

Conditional on $v_{T_1} = v$, the log-return $X = \ln R$ has the **same distributional form** as a standard Heston log-return over the interval $[T_1, T]$ with initial variance $v$:

$$
\phi_{X|v}(u) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{iu X} \mid v_{T_1} = v\right] = \exp\!\left(C(\tau, u) + D(\tau, u) \, v\right)
$$

where $\tau = T - T_1$ and $C, D$ are the standard Heston Riccati solutions with parameters $\kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho$ and initial condition (the risk-neutral drift is absorbed into $C$).

!!! note "Theorem (Forward-Start Pricing via Iterated Expectation)"
    The forward-start call price under Heston is:

    $$
    V_0 = S_0 e^{-qT_1} \, \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{-r\tau} \, \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[(R - \alpha)^+ \mid v_{T_1}\right]\right]
    $$

    where the inner expectation is a Heston call price with spot $= 1$, strike $= \alpha$, maturity $= \tau$, and initial variance $= v_{T_1}$:

    $$
    \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[(R - \alpha)^+ \mid v_{T_1}\right] = C_{\text{Heston}}(1, \alpha, r, q, \tau, v_{T_1}, \kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho)
    $$

    The outer expectation is taken over $v_{T_1}$, whose distribution is non-central chi-squared (CIR transition density).

### Pricing Algorithm

**Step 1.** For a grid of variance values $v^{(1)}, v^{(2)}, \ldots, v^{(M)}$, compute the Heston call price $C_{\text{Heston}}(1, \alpha, \tau, v^{(m)})$ using the COS method or Gil-Pelaez inversion.

**Step 2.** Compute the outer expectation by integrating against the CIR transition density of $v_{T_1}$:

$$
V_0 = S_0 e^{-qT_1} e^{-r\tau} \int_0^{\infty} C_{\text{Heston}}(1, \alpha, \tau, v) \, f_{v_{T_1}}(v \mid v_0) \, dv
$$

where $f_{v_{T_1}}$ is the non-central chi-squared density of $v_{T_1}$.

**Step 3.** The integral is computed numerically using Gauss-Laguerre quadrature (exploiting the semi-infinite domain and exponential decay of $f_{v_{T_1}}$) or adaptive quadrature.

!!! tip "Alternative: Joint Fourier Method"
    An alternative approach avoids the two-step integration by using the **joint characteristic function** of $(v_{T_1}, X)$. Since the Heston model is affine in $(v, X)$, the joint CF has an exponential-affine form, and the forward-start price can be expressed as a single two-dimensional Fourier integral. This is more elegant but computationally heavier than the iterated approach for a single maturity.

## Forward Implied Volatility Smile

### Definition

The **forward smile** is the Black--Scholes implied volatility of the forward-start option as a function of the moneyness ratio $\alpha$. Specifically, for each $\alpha$:

1. Compute $V_0(\alpha)$ using the Heston forward-start pricing formula
2. Solve for $\sigma_{\text{fwd}}(\alpha)$ such that the Black--Scholes forward-start price (with $\sigma = \sigma_{\text{fwd}}$) equals $V_0(\alpha)$

### Properties Under Heston

The forward smile under Heston has distinctive features:

1. **Flattening with $T_1$**: As the strike-setting date $T_1$ moves further into the future, the forward smile flattens. This occurs because $v_{T_1}$ regresses toward $\theta$ (mean reversion), reducing the impact of the current variance state $v_0$.

2. **Level set by $\theta$**: For large $T_1$, the forward ATM implied volatility converges to $\sqrt{\theta}$, the long-run volatility, regardless of $v_0$.

3. **Skew persistence**: The skew (slope of the smile at $\alpha = 1$) is controlled by $\rho$. Under Heston, the forward skew decreases with $T_1$ but remains non-zero, reflecting the persistent role of correlation.

4. **Curvature from $\xi$**: The vol-of-vol $\xi$ controls the curvature of the forward smile. Higher $\xi$ produces more curvature (wider smile) through the increased dispersion of $v_{T_1}$.

!!! warning "Forward Smile and Local Volatility"
    Under local volatility models (e.g., Dupire), the forward smile is **flat** --- all forward-start options at the same moneyness have the same implied volatility. This is because local volatility is a deterministic function of $(S, t)$, and the forward return's distribution is independent of $S_{T_1}$ once conditioned on $(S_{T_1}, T_1)$, with no stochastic state variable. The non-flat forward smile under Heston is a key distinguishing feature of stochastic volatility models and is used in practice to differentiate local vol from stochastic vol.

## Connection to Cliquet Contracts

### Cliquet Definition

A **cliquet** (ratchet) option is a series of consecutive forward-start options with returns capped and/or floored. A typical cliquet pays:

$$
\Phi_{\text{cliquet}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \min\!\left(c, \max\!\left(f, \frac{S_{T_i} - S_{T_{i-1}}}{S_{T_{i-1}}}\right)\right)
$$

where $c$ is the cap, $f$ is the floor (often $f = 0$), and $T_0 < T_1 < \cdots < T_n$ are the reset dates.

### Pricing Under Heston

Each cliquet period is a capped/floored forward-start option. However, the periods are **not independent** under Heston because the variance at the end of one period ($v_{T_i}$) is the initial variance for the next. The pricing must account for this dependence.

Two approaches are common:

1. **Monte Carlo**: Simulate the full joint path $(S_t, v_t)$ across all periods. This is straightforward but computationally expensive for long-dated cliquets (5--10 years with monthly resets).

2. **Recursive Fourier method**: Price each period backward using the conditional characteristic function, passing the distribution of $v_{T_i}$ from one period to the next. This leverages the affine structure of Heston.

!!! tip "Why Heston Matters for Cliquets"
    Cliquets are among the most model-sensitive exotic derivatives. The total payoff depends on the joint distribution of returns across all periods, which is heavily influenced by the **forward smile dynamics**. Local volatility models underprice cliquets because their flat forward smile underestimates the dispersion of periodic returns. Heston's non-flat forward smile captures the key source of value --- the stochastic volatility effect --- making it a much better pricing model for cliquets.

## Worked Example

### Parameters

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| $S_0$ | \$100 |
| $\alpha$ | 1.0 (ATM) |
| $T_1$ | 6 months (strike-setting date) |
| $T$ | 1 year (maturity) |
| $r$ | 3% |
| $q$ | 0% |
| $v_0$ | 0.04 |
| $\kappa$ | 2.0 |
| $\theta$ | 0.04 |
| $\xi$ | 0.5 |
| $\rho$ | $-0.7$ |

### Forward Smile

| Moneyness $\alpha$ | Forward-Start Call | Forward IV |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| 0.90 | \$7.38 | 22.1% |
| 0.95 | \$4.88 | 21.2% |
| 1.00 | \$2.98 | 20.5% |
| 1.05 | \$1.63 | 20.1% |
| 1.10 | \$0.80 | 20.0% |

!!! example "Observations"
    1. The forward smile is **negatively skewed**: lower moneyness ratios (OTM puts) have higher implied volatility, consistent with the negative $\rho = -0.7$.
    2. The forward ATM implied volatility (20.5%) is close to $\sqrt{\theta} = 20\%$ because the 6-month delay allows significant mean reversion ($e^{-2 \times 0.5} = e^{-1} \approx 0.37$).
    3. Under Black--Scholes, all forward IVs would be identically 20% (the constant vol). The spread of 2.1% across strikes (from 20.0% to 22.1%) quantifies the stochastic volatility effect on the forward smile.

## Summary

| Concept | Formula / Description |
|---------|-----------------------|
| Forward-start call payoff | $(S_T - \alpha S_{T_1})^+$ |
| BS independence | $R = S_T/S_{T_1}$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_{T_1}$ |
| Heston conditional CF | $\phi_{X|v}(u) = \exp(C(\tau,u) + D(\tau,u)v)$ |
| Pricing via iterated $\mathbb{E}$ | $V_0 = S_0 e^{-qT_1-r\tau}\int_0^{\infty} C_{\text{Heston}}(1,\alpha,\tau,v)f_{v_{T_1}}(v)dv$ |
| Forward smile | Non-flat under Heston; flat under local vol |

!!! abstract "Key Takeaways"
    1. **Conditional CF preserves affine structure**: Given $v_{T_1}$, the forward return has the same Heston CF structure, enabling semi-analytical inner pricing.

    2. **Two-step integration**: Price by first computing the Heston call for each $v_{T_1}$ value, then averaging over the CIR distribution of $v_{T_1}$.

    3. **Forward smile is non-flat**: Unlike local volatility, Heston generates a forward smile controlled by $\rho$ (skew), $\xi$ (curvature), and $\kappa$ (flattening with $T_1$).

    4. **Cliquet pricing**: Forward-start options are building blocks of cliquets; the dependence of periodic returns through $v_{T_i}$ requires either MC or recursive Fourier methods.

    5. **Key differentiator**: The forward smile is a powerful diagnostic for distinguishing stochastic volatility from local volatility models.
