# Chapter 17: Calibration

This chapter treats model calibration as an inverse problem: given observed market prices, recover model parameters that reproduce them. Starting from the forward pricing map and its inversion, we develop the mathematical framework for ill-posedness and regularization, apply it to local and stochastic volatility calibration, address the dynamic consistency of recalibration over time, and quantify the model risk that calibration uncertainty introduces into pricing and hedging.

## Key Concepts

**Calibration as an Inverse Problem**
A parametric model with parameter vector $\theta \in \Theta \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ defines a **forward pricing map** $F:\Theta \to \mathbb{R}^m$ that sends parameters to model prices across $m$ traded instruments. Calibration seeks the inverse: $\hat{\theta} \in \arg\min_\theta \mathcal{L}(F(\theta), y)$ where $y$ is market data and $\mathcal{L}$ is a loss function. The Jacobian $J(\theta) = \nabla_\theta F(\theta)$ governs local sensitivity; small singular values of $J$ amplify data noise into large parameter errors, making the inverse unstable. **Identifiability**—whether the data uniquely determine parameters—depends on the Jacobian rank, with the condition number $\kappa = \sigma_1/\sigma_d$ measuring sensitivity. Calibration is typically **ill-posed** in the sense of Hadamard: uniqueness and stability can both fail due to incomplete information (finite instruments vs. functional degrees of freedom), noisy data (bid–ask spreads, stale quotes), and model misspecification ($y \notin \text{Range}(F)$). Non-uniqueness arises from flat directions in the loss landscape (parameter degeneracy), over-parameterization, and multiple local minima. Optimization algorithms range from **Levenberg–Marquardt** $(J^\top WJ + \lambda I)\Delta\theta = -J^\top Wr$ (the standard workhorse, interpolating between Gauss–Newton and gradient descent) and trust-region methods, to global approaches including differential evolution, particle swarm, simulated annealing, and basin hopping. Derivative-free methods (Nelder–Mead, Bayesian optimization) are essential when pricing involves Monte Carlo noise.

**Static Calibration to Vanilla Options**
Calibration targets either option prices or **implied volatilities** $\sigma_{\text{impl}}(K,T)$. Price-space objectives align with replication cost but suffer from heterogeneous magnitudes; implied-vol objectives provide uniform scaling and match market conventions but implicitly re-weight errors through vega. The statistical interpretation as maximum likelihood under $\varepsilon_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_j^2)$ motivates weights $w_j = 1/\sigma_j^2$, with bid–ask spreads providing a noise proxy. Robust alternatives—Huber loss, $\ell_1$ loss—reduce sensitivity to outliers and stale quotes. **Weighting and liquidity** are critical: near-the-money options are most liquid, far OTM quotes may be informative but noisy, and maturity balancing prevents one tenor from dominating. The implied volatility surface is typically constructed in log-moneyness coordinates with arbitrage filtering (calendar monotonicity in total variance, butterfly convexity in strike) before calibration proceeds.

**Regularization and Stability**
Tikhonov regularization $\min_\theta \frac{1}{2}\|F(\theta)-y\|_W^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|L(\theta-\theta_0)\|^2$ stabilizes ill-conditioned problems by damping small singular values, trading bias for variance reduction. It admits a **Bayesian interpretation** as the MAP estimator under a Gaussian prior $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta_0, (\lambda L^\top L)^{-1})$. Selection of the regularization strength $\lambda$ uses the L-curve method, discrepancy principle, or cross-validation. Beyond parameter shrinkage, **penalization and smoothness constraints** encode structural beliefs: finite-difference penalties on roughness, monotonicity and convexity constraints motivated by no-arbitrage, and positivity requirements. The fundamental **bias–robustness trade-off** governs all calibration: perfect in-sample fit is neither achievable nor desirable; robust calibration prioritizes stable parameters, economically plausible dynamics, and reliable Greeks over minimal residuals. Time stability—smooth day-to-day parameter evolution without market justification—is often the decisive practical criterion.

**Calibration of Local Volatility**
The local volatility model $dS_t = (r-q)S_t\,dt + \sigma_{\text{loc}}(t,S_t)S_t\,dW_t$ achieves exact fit to a continuum of vanilla prices, but Dupire's inversion $\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2 = 2(\partial_T C + (r-q)K\partial_K C - qC)/(K^2\partial_{KK}C)$ is a **highly ill-posed inverse problem** because it requires differentiating noisy, discrete data. Noise amplification scales as $1/h^2$ for second derivatives, and division by small curvature $\partial_{KK}C$ in the wings amplifies errors further. Stability requires smoothing before differentiation: **SVI parameterization** $w(k) = a + b(\rho(k-m) + \sqrt{(k-m)^2+\sigma^2})$ provides a parsimonious 5-parameter smile fit with analytic derivatives, while **SSVI** extends to the full $(k,T)$ surface with built-in calendar arbitrage freedom. Numerical differentiation best practices include central differences, Richardson extrapolation (on smooth data), spline-based analytic differentiation, and working in total variance coordinates $w = T\sigma_{\text{impl}}^2$ where calendar arbitrage reduces to $\partial_T w \geq 0$. Instability concentrates near extreme strikes, sparse maturities, and short expiries where microstructure and discrete dividends distort $\partial_T C$.

**Dynamic Calibration and Time Consistency**
Models recalibrated daily face the **recalibration problem**: parameter shifts $\theta_t \to \theta_{t+\Delta t}$ may be inconsistent with the model's own dynamics, generating unexplained P&L and destabilizing hedges. Sources include genuine regime shifts, quote noise, model misspecification, and optimizer sensitivity. **Forward consistency** requires that model-implied parameter evolution match fresh recalibration: $\hat{\theta}_{t+\Delta t}^{\text{calib}} \approx \Phi_{t \to t+\Delta t}(\hat{\theta}_t, \omega)$. The HJM framework achieves this for interest rates by modeling the full forward curve as the state variable, but low-dimensional equity models (Heston, SABR) are structurally forward-inconsistent. Practitioner responses include parameter smoothing, freezing slowly-moving parameters, hierarchical calibration, and Tikhonov regularization toward prior values. Distinguishing **model drift** (misspecification artifacts) from **parameter drift** (genuine economic variation) uses encompassing tests, out-of-sample stability, specification tests, and cross-sectional consistency. **Filtering** provides a principled alternative: the state-space formulation with Kalman, extended Kalman, unscented Kalman, or particle filters produces smooth parameter paths with automatic uncertainty quantification, replacing ad-hoc recalibration with Bayesian-optimal sequential estimation.

**Model Risk from Calibration**
Parameter error propagates to prices via $\Delta P \approx -\nabla_\theta P^\top \Delta\theta$, with price variance $\text{Var}(P) \approx \nabla_\theta P^\top \Sigma_\theta \nabla_\theta P$ where $\Sigma_\theta$ is the parameter covariance (from Hessian, bootstrap, or Bayesian posterior). Exotic options amplify calibration error due to path dependence and sensitivity to weakly identified parameters—a barrier option may exhibit $5$–$10\%$ price uncertainty from $5\%$ correlation error, far exceeding vanilla sensitivity. Greeks are equally affected: $\Delta(\hat{\theta}) \neq \Delta(\theta^\star)$ produces systematic hedging errors that accumulate linearly over time. **Robust calibration criteria** combine stability under data perturbations, avoidance of extreme parameters, out-of-sample fit, temporal smoothness, and stable Greeks. Practical tools include bump-and-reprice sensitivity, bootstrap distributions, cross-validation, and model comparison across Heston, SABR, and local volatility to quantify model uncertainty beyond parameter uncertainty.

**Stochastic Volatility Calibration Pipelines**
Heston calibration typically uses semi-analytic FFT pricing with Levenberg–Marquardt optimization, requiring careful initialization due to non-convexity and parameter entanglement between $\kappa$ and $\bar{v}$. SABR calibration leverages Hagan's closed-form approximation for rapid per-slice fits. Differential evolution and other global search methods address multi-modality. Cross-model comparison reveals complementary strengths: SABR excels for liquid standard products, Heston for path-dependent exotics, and local volatility for exact surface fit—with ensemble approaches and cross-model Greek comparison providing robustness checks.
