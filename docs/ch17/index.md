# Chapter 17: Calibration

This chapter treats model calibration as an inverse problem: given observed market prices, recover model parameters that reproduce them. Starting from the forward pricing map and its inversion, we develop the mathematical framework for ill-posedness and regularization, apply it to local and stochastic volatility calibration, address the dynamic consistency of recalibration over time, and quantify the model risk that calibration uncertainty introduces into pricing and hedging. The chapter covers the full calibration pipeline---from objective function design and optimization algorithms through regularization, dynamic recalibration, filtering, and model risk governance---providing a unified treatment of both theoretical foundations and practical implementation.

## Key Concepts

**Calibration as an Inverse Problem**
A parametric model with parameter vector $\theta \in \Theta \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ defines a **forward pricing map** $F:\Theta \to \mathbb{R}^m$ that sends parameters to model prices across $m$ traded instruments: $F(\theta) = (P_1(\theta), \ldots, P_m(\theta))$. Calibration seeks the inverse: $\hat{\theta} \in \arg\min_\theta \mathcal{L}(F(\theta), y)$ where $y$ is market data and $\mathcal{L}$ is a loss function. The **inverse calibration map** $\theta = F^{-1}(y)$ is rarely globally invertible; instead, calibration is formulated as optimization. The Jacobian $J(\theta) = \nabla_\theta F(\theta) \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d}$ governs local sensitivity via the linearization $F(\theta_0 + \Delta\theta) \approx F(\theta_0) + J(\theta_0)\Delta\theta$; small singular values of $J$ amplify data noise into large parameter errors, making the inverse unstable. **Identifiability**---whether the data uniquely determine parameters---is analyzed via the SVD $J = U\Sigma V^\top$: the model is **structurally identifiable** if $F(\theta_1) = F(\theta_2) \Rightarrow \theta_1 = \theta_2$, and **practically identifiable** when parameters are distinguishable within the noise level. The condition number $\kappa = \sigma_1/\sigma_d$ measures sensitivity, with very small $\sigma_d$ indicating a nearly unidentifiable direction. Identifiability is improved by including options across a range of strikes and maturities (short maturities pin down spot volatility; long maturities identify mean reversion and long-run variance), re-parameterization to more observable quantities, and regularization via Bayesian priors. Calibration is typically **ill-posed** in the sense of Hadamard: existence, uniqueness, and stability can all fail. The three failure mechanisms are: (1) **incomplete information**---finite instruments versus functional degrees of freedom, with effective Jacobian rank much smaller than $d$; (2) **noisy data**---bid--ask spreads, stale quotes, and microstructure noise that are amplified through the unstable inverse $\Delta\theta \approx (J^\top WJ)^{-1}J^\top W(y - F(\theta_0))$; and (3) **model misspecification**---$y \notin \text{Range}(F)$. Non-uniqueness arises from flat directions in the loss landscape (parameter degeneracy), over-parameterization producing extreme or day-to-day unstable parameter values, and multiple local minima created by constraints (positivity, Feller condition, no-arbitrage filters).

**Optimization Algorithms for Calibration**
The calibration optimization problem $\hat{\theta} \in \arg\min_{\theta \in \Theta} \mathcal{L}(\theta)$ with weighted least-squares loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\|r(\theta)\|_W^2$ may include constraints $g_i(\theta) \le 0$ (positivity, Feller condition, correlation bounds). **Local methods** include: gradient descent $\theta^{(k+1)} = \theta^{(k)} - \alpha_k \nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta^{(k)})$ (simple but slow); the Gauss--Newton method $(J^\top WJ)\Delta\theta = -J^\top Wr$ (effective for mildly nonlinear problems); the **Levenberg--Marquardt** algorithm $(J^\top WJ + \lambda I)\Delta\theta = -J^\top Wr$ (the standard workhorse, interpolating between Gauss--Newton for small $\lambda$ and gradient descent for large $\lambda$, with adaptive damping based on step acceptance); and **trust-region methods** that constrain $\|\Delta\theta\| \le \Delta_k$ with radius adjusted by the actual-to-predicted reduction ratio $\rho_k$. **Global methods** address multiple local minima: **differential evolution** creates mutants $v_i = \theta_{r_1} + F(\theta_{r_2} - \theta_{r_3})$ with mutation factor $F \in (0,2]$ and crossover; **particle swarm optimization** updates velocities $v_i \leftarrow \omega v_i + c_1 r_1(p_i - \theta_i) + c_2 r_2(g - \theta_i)$ balancing cognitive and social components; **simulated annealing** accepts worse solutions with probability $\exp(-\Delta\mathcal{L}/T)$ under a cooling schedule; and **basin hopping** combines local optimization with random perturbations and Metropolis acceptance. **Derivative-free methods**---Nelder--Mead simplex, Powell's conjugate directions, and **Bayesian optimization** (Gaussian process surrogate with expected improvement acquisition)---are essential when pricing involves Monte Carlo noise. Constraint handling uses projection, barrier/interior-point methods, sigmoid transformations $\theta = \theta_{\min} + (\theta_{\max} - \theta_{\min})\sigma(\phi)$, penalty methods, augmented Lagrangian, or sequential quadratic programming. Convergence diagnostics combine gradient norm $\|\nabla\mathcal{L}\| < \epsilon_g$, relative parameter change, relative objective change, and maximum iteration limits. Computational cost depends critically on the pricing method: $\mathcal{O}(1)$ for Black--Scholes, $\mathcal{O}(N_{\text{FFT}})$ for Heston via FFT, $\mathcal{O}(N_{\text{paths}} \times N_{\text{steps}})$ for Monte Carlo.

**Static Calibration to Vanilla Options**
Calibration targets either option prices or **implied volatilities** $\sigma_{\text{impl}}(K,T)$. The **choice of objective function** involves fundamental trade-offs: price-space objectives $\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_j w_j(P_j(\theta) - P_j^{\text{mkt}})^2$ align with replication cost but suffer from heterogeneous magnitudes (deep ITM options dominate); implied-vol objectives $\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_j w_j(\sigma_j^{\text{impl}}(\theta) - \sigma_j^{\text{impl,mkt}})^2$ provide uniform scaling and match market conventions but implicitly re-weight errors through vega. The statistical interpretation as maximum likelihood under $\varepsilon_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_j^2)$ motivates weights $w_j = 1/\sigma_j^2$, with bid--ask spreads providing a noise proxy. **Robust loss functions** reduce sensitivity to outliers: Huber loss $\ell_H(r) = \frac{1}{2}r^2$ for $|r| \le \delta$ and $\delta|r| - \frac{1}{2}\delta^2$ for $|r| > \delta$; $\ell_1$ loss $|r|$; and multi-objective formulations balancing fit quality against parameter plausibility. **Weighting and market liquidity** are critical: near-the-money options are most liquid and should receive higher weight; vega-weighted objectives $w_j \propto 1/\nu_j^2$ normalize for option price sensitivity; far OTM quotes may be informative but noisy; and maturity balancing prevents one tenor from dominating. The **implied volatility surface** is typically constructed in log-moneyness coordinates $k = \log(K/F_T)$ with arbitrage filtering---calendar monotonicity in total variance $\partial_T w \ge 0$ and butterfly convexity in strike $\partial_{KK}C \ge 0$---before calibration proceeds.

**Regularization and Stability**
**Tikhonov regularization** $\min_\theta \frac{1}{2}\|F(\theta)-y\|_W^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|L(\theta-\theta_0)\|^2$ stabilizes ill-conditioned problems by damping small singular values, trading bias for variance reduction. It admits a **Bayesian interpretation** as the MAP estimator under a Gaussian prior $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta_0, (\lambda L^\top L)^{-1})$, connecting regularization strength to prior certainty. Selection of $\lambda$ uses: the **L-curve method** (corner of log-residual vs. log-penalty plot), the **discrepancy principle** (choose $\lambda$ so residual matches noise level), or **cross-validation** (hold-out prediction error). Beyond parameter shrinkage, **penalization and smoothness constraints** encode structural beliefs: finite-difference penalties on parameter roughness $\lambda\sum_i(\theta_{i+1} - \theta_i)^2$, monotonicity and convexity constraints motivated by no-arbitrage, and positivity requirements. Shape constraints are implemented via constrained optimization, barrier functions, or parameter transformations that enforce constraints by construction. The fundamental **bias--robustness trade-off** governs all calibration: perfect in-sample fit is neither achievable nor desirable; robust calibration prioritizes stable parameters, economically plausible dynamics, and reliable Greeks over minimal residuals. **Time stability**---smooth day-to-day parameter evolution via regularization toward yesterday's parameters $\lambda\|\theta - \hat{\theta}_{t-1}\|^2$---is often the decisive practical criterion, with the optimal $\lambda$ balancing responsiveness to genuine market moves against noise filtering.

**Calibration of Local Volatility**
The local volatility model $dS_t = (r-q)S_t\,dt + \sigma_{\text{loc}}(t,S_t)S_t\,dW_t$ achieves exact fit to a continuum of vanilla prices, but **Dupire's inversion** $\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2 = 2(\partial_T C + (r-q)K\partial_K C - qC)/(K^2\partial_{KK}C)$ is a **highly ill-posed inverse problem** because it requires differentiating noisy, discrete data. For second derivatives, noise amplification scales as $\sigma_\varepsilon/h^2$, where the central difference approximation $\partial_{KK}C \approx (C(K+h) - 2C(K) + C(K-h))/h^2$ has an optimal step size $h^* \sim (\sigma_\varepsilon/|C^{(4)}|)^{1/4}$ balancing noise amplification against truncation error. Higher-accuracy stencils such as the five-point formula achieve $\mathcal{O}(h^4)$ accuracy. **Richardson extrapolation** $A_{\text{extrap}} = (2^p A(h/2) - A(h))/(2^p - 1)$ improves accuracy but amplifies errors on noisy data. **Spline-based differentiation**---fitting cubic or smoothing splines $\min\sum_i(S(K_i) - C_i)^2 + \lambda\int(S''(K))^2\,dK$ and differentiating analytically---avoids step-size selection and handles non-uniform strike spacing. Working in **total variance coordinates** $w(k,T) = T\sigma_{\text{impl}}^2(k,T)$ with $k = \log(K/F_T)$ simplifies the Dupire formula and makes calendar arbitrage equivalent to $\partial_T w \ge 0$. **SVI parameterization** $w(k) = a + b(\rho(k-m) + \sqrt{(k-m)^2+\sigma^2})$ provides a parsimonious 5-parameter smile fit with analytic derivatives $\partial_k w = b(\rho + (k-m)/\sqrt{(k-m)^2+\sigma^2})$ and $\partial_{kk}w = b\sigma^2/((k-m)^2+\sigma^2)^{3/2}$. Butterfly-free sufficient conditions include $b(1+|\rho|) \le 4/T$ and $a + b\sigma\sqrt{1-\rho^2} \ge 0$. **SSVI (Surface SVI)** $w(k,\theta_T) = \frac{\theta_T}{2}(1 + \rho\phi(\theta_T)k + \sqrt{(\phi(\theta_T)k + \rho)^2 + 1 - \rho^2})$ extends to the full $(k,T)$ surface with calendar arbitrage freedom built in by construction, using power-law $\phi(\theta) = \eta/(\theta^\gamma(1+\theta)^{1-\gamma})$ or Heston-like functional forms. Alternative SVI parameterizations include natural SVI (decoupling ATM behavior) and jump-wings SVI (parameterized by observable quantities: ATM variance, ATM skew, wing slopes). Instability concentrates near extreme strikes (where $\partial_{KK}C \to 0$), sparse maturities, and short expiries where microstructure and discrete dividends distort $\partial_T C$.

**Dynamic Calibration and Time Consistency**
Models recalibrated daily face the **recalibration problem**: parameter shifts $\theta_t \to \theta_{t+\Delta t}$ may be inconsistent with the model's own dynamics, generating unexplained P&L $\text{residual} = \text{P\&L} - (\Delta\cdot\Delta S + \frac{1}{2}\Gamma\cdot(\Delta S)^2 + \text{Vega}\cdot\Delta\sigma + \Theta\cdot\Delta t)$ and destabilizing hedges. Sources include genuine regime shifts, quote noise, model misspecification, ill-posed calibration objectives amplifying noise via small Jacobian singular values, and numerical optimizer sensitivity to initialization. Quantitative diagnostics include day-over-day parameter changes $\Delta\theta_t = \hat{\theta}_t - \hat{\theta}_{t-1}$, rolling parameter volatility, bootstrap sensitivity (perturbing quotes within bid--ask and recalibrating), objective function landscape analysis (profile likelihood, one-parameter sweeps), and out-of-sample fit. **Forward consistency** requires that model-implied parameter evolution match fresh recalibration: $\hat{\theta}_{t+\Delta t}^{\text{calib}} \approx \Phi_{t \to t+\Delta t}(\hat{\theta}_t, \omega)$. The **HJM framework** achieves this for interest rates by modeling the entire forward rate curve $df(t,T) = \alpha(t,T)\,dt + \sigma(t,T)\,dW_t$ as the state variable, with the no-arbitrage drift condition $\alpha(t,T) = \sigma(t,T)\int_t^T \sigma(t,u)\,du$ ensuring that recalibration is unnecessary for the curve shape. Low-dimensional equity models (Heston, SABR) are structurally forward-inconsistent because the 5-parameter state cannot capture arbitrary surface dynamics. Approaches to restore consistency include: **state-extended models** such as Bergomi's variance curve model $d\xi_t(T) = \eta(T-t)\xi_t(T)\,dW_t$ which models the forward variance curve as the state; **stochastic parameter models** $d\theta_t = \mu_\theta(\theta_t)\,dt + \Sigma_\theta(\theta_t)\,dZ_t$; term-structure extensions replacing scalar parameters with maturity-dependent functions; the **consistent recalibration (CRC) framework** (Bjork--Landen--Svensson, Carmona--Nadtochiy) requiring $\mathcal{C}(\text{prices at }t+\Delta t) = \Phi(\mathcal{C}(\text{prices at }t), \omega)$; and market models for volatility instruments (variance swaps, VIX futures). Practitioner responses include exponential parameter smoothing $\theta_t^{\text{smooth}} = \lambda\hat{\theta}_t + (1-\lambda)\theta_{t-1}^{\text{smooth}}$, freezing slowly-moving parameters ($\kappa$, $\bar{v}$) while recalibrating fast-moving ones ($v_0$, $\rho$) daily, hierarchical calibration aligning parameter time scales with economic intuition, Tikhonov regularization toward prior values $\min_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta) + \lambda\|\theta - \hat{\theta}_{t-1}\|^2$, and regime-switching models. Distinguishing **model drift** (misspecification artifacts) from **parameter drift** (genuine economic variation) uses encompassing tests, out-of-sample stability, specification tests, cross-sectional consistency, and quantitative decomposition of parameter change into market-explained and residual components.

**Filtering for Time-Varying Parameters**
**Filtering** provides a principled alternative to ad-hoc recalibration by treating parameters as time-varying state variables in a **state-space formulation**: state equation $\theta_{t+1} = f(\theta_t) + \eta_t$ with $\eta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,Q)$ and observation equation $y_t = h(\theta_t) + \varepsilon_t$ with $\varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,R)$, where $h$ is the pricing function and $Q$, $R$ encode parameter uncertainty growth and market data noise respectively. The **Kalman filter** provides exact updates for linear-Gaussian systems: predict $\hat{\theta}_{t|t-1} = A\hat{\theta}_{t-1|t-1}$, $P_{t|t-1} = AP_{t-1|t-1}A^\top + Q$; update via Kalman gain $K_t = P_{t|t-1}H^\top(HP_{t|t-1}H^\top + R)^{-1}$ giving $\hat{\theta}_{t|t} = \hat{\theta}_{t|t-1} + K_t(y_t - H\hat{\theta}_{t|t-1})$. The **extended Kalman filter (EKF)** handles nonlinear $h(\theta)$ by linearizing $H_t = \nabla_\theta h(\hat{\theta}_{t|t-1})$ at the current estimate, achieving first-order accuracy with computational cost comparable to optimization-based calibration. The **unscented Kalman filter (UKF)** propagates $2d+1$ sigma points $\chi_i = \hat{\theta} \pm \sqrt{(d+\lambda)P}|_i$ through nonlinear functions, capturing second-order accuracy without explicit Jacobian computation. **Particle filters** (sequential Monte Carlo) represent the posterior $p(\theta_t \mid y_{1:t})$ with weighted samples: predict by propagating particles through state dynamics, update weights via likelihood $w_t^{(i)} \propto p(y_t \mid \tilde{\theta}_t^{(i)})$, and resample to avoid degeneracy. For the **Heston model application**, the augmented state vector $\theta_t = (v_t, \kappa, \bar{v}, \sigma_v, \rho)^\top$ typically uses a random walk state equation with $Q$ encoding beliefs about parameter stability (small entries for $\kappa$, $\bar{v}$; larger entries for $v_0$, $\rho$). Rao--Blackwellization exploits partial linear-Gaussian structure for efficiency. Filter hyperparameters $Q$ and $R$ are estimated via maximum likelihood, cross-validation, or expert judgment. Filtering produces smooth parameter paths with automatic uncertainty quantification, replacing ad-hoc recalibration with Bayesian-optimal sequential estimation.

**Model Risk from Calibration**
Parameter error propagates to prices via the first-order approximation $\Delta P \approx -\nabla_\theta P(\hat{\theta})^\top \Delta\theta$, with price variance $\text{Var}(P) \approx \nabla_\theta P^\top \Sigma_\theta \nabla_\theta P$ where $\Sigma_\theta$ is the parameter covariance estimated from the Hessian of the calibration objective, bootstrap, or Bayesian posterior. Second-order effects $\Delta P \approx \nabla_\theta P^\top\Delta\theta + \frac{1}{2}\Delta\theta^\top H_P \Delta\theta$ matter for exotics with non-monotonic parameter dependence; positive-definite $H_P$ creates upward bias via Jensen's inequality $\mathbb{E}[P(\theta)] \ge P(\mathbb{E}[\theta])$. Price variance decomposes by parameter: $\text{Contribution}_i = (\partial P/\partial\theta_i)^2\sigma_{\theta_i}^2$, with spot volatility $v_0$ dominating short-dated options, mean reversion $\kappa$ affecting term structure products, and correlation $\rho$ critical for skew-sensitive instruments. **Exotic options amplify calibration error**: a barrier option may exhibit $5$--$10\%$ price uncertainty from $5\%$ correlation error, far exceeding vanilla sensitivity, due to path dependence and sensitivity to weakly identified parameters. **Parameter VaR** $\text{VaR}_\alpha^{\text{param}} = z_\alpha\cdot\sigma_P$ provides confidence intervals, with model reserves $\text{Reserve} = \lambda\cdot\text{VaR}_\alpha^{\text{param}}$ held by banks. Greeks are equally affected: $\Delta(\hat{\theta}) \neq \Delta(\theta^\star)$ produces systematic hedging errors that accumulate linearly over time, with the sensitivity of delta to parameters $\nabla_\theta\Delta = \partial^2 P/(\partial S\partial\theta)$ governing the distortion of hedge ratios. Higher-order Greeks (gamma, vanna, volga) are often more sensitive to parameters than delta. Hedging breakdown mechanisms include systematic hedge ratio errors creating unintended directional exposure, hedge ratio volatility from unstable recalibration increasing transaction costs, and cross-Greek contamination where mis-estimated correlation $\rho$ distorts both delta and vega simultaneously. **Robust calibration criteria** combine: (1) stability under data perturbations $\|\theta(\text{data}+\epsilon) - \theta(\text{data})\| \le C\|\epsilon\|$; (2) avoidance of extreme parameters; (3) reasonable out-of-sample performance via cross-validation; (4) temporal smoothness of parameters; and (5) stable Greeks and hedging behavior. Robust objective functions include regularized losses $\mathcal{L}_{\text{robust}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{fit}} + \lambda\mathcal{R}(\theta)$, worst-case objectives $\min_\theta\max_{y \in \mathcal{Y}}\mathcal{L}(\theta,y)$, and ensemble calibration aggregating multiple runs. Practical measurement tools include bump-and-reprice sensitivity (with bump size $\delta \sim \sigma_{\theta_i}$), scenario analysis under alternative calibrations, alternative model comparison (Heston vs. SABR vs. local vol), and bootstrap distributions. **Model risk governance** under regulatory expectations (OCC SR 11-7) requires sensitivity analysis, documentation of parameter uncertainty, position limits for high-model-sensitivity products, reserves for hedging error, and ongoing monitoring of calibration performance.

**Stochastic Volatility Calibration Pipelines**
Heston calibration typically uses semi-analytic FFT pricing with Levenberg--Marquardt optimization, requiring careful initialization due to non-convexity and parameter entanglement between $\kappa$ and $\bar{v}$. SABR calibration leverages Hagan's closed-form approximation $dF_t = \alpha_t F_t^\beta dB_t^F$, $d\alpha_t = \nu\alpha_t dB_t^\alpha$ for rapid per-slice fits with 4 intuitive parameters, excelling near ATM but less accurate at far OTM strikes. Differential evolution and other global search methods address multi-modality. **Cross-model comparison** reveals complementary strengths: SABR provides excellent ATM and smile fit with good parameter stability; Heston offers analytical dynamics for path-dependent exotics with moderate ATM and term structure fit but challenging parameter stability; local volatility achieves perfect fit by construction (no parameters) but suffers from poor extrapolation and lacks mean-reversion dynamics. A practical calibration pipeline starts with SABR for speed and stability, validates with Heston for dynamics and path-dependence, uses local vol for exotic pricing, compares Greeks across models for robustness, and selects the model based on product type and market regime---with ensemble approaches and cross-model Greek comparison providing robustness checks.
